Acute Toxicity Calculations

As defined in GHS Rev. 10, 2023, acute toxicity refers to serious adverse health effects (i.e. lethality) occuring after a single or short-term oral, dermal or inhalation exposure to a substance or mixture.
Acute Toxicity Calculations in this calculator are based on numerical Acute Toxicity Estimates (ATE), which can be seen as a proxy for the lethal dose or concentration for 50% of the test animals (respectively called LD50 and LC50).

In the table below, the ATE thresholds for the various exposures can be found.

Exposure Route Category 1 Category 2 Category 3 Category 4
Oral (mg/kg bodyweight) ≤ 5 > 5 - ATE ≤ 50 > 50 - ATE ≤ 300 > 300 - ATE ≤ 2000
Dermal (mg/kg bodyweight) ≤ 50 > 50 - ATE ≤ 200 > 200 - ATE ≤ 1000 > 1000 - ATE ≤ 2000
Inhalation, Gas (ppmV) ≤ 100 > 100 - ATE ≤ 500 > 500 - ATE ≤ 2500 > 2500 - ATE ≤ 20000
Inhalation, Vapours (mg/L) ≤ 0.5 > 0.5 - ATE ≤ 2 > 2 - ATE ≤ 10 > 10 - ATE ≤ 20
Inhalation, Mist (mg/L) ≤ 0.05 > 0.05 - ATE ≤ 0.5 > 0.5 - ATE ≤ 1 > 1 - ATE ≤ 5

To obtain the ATE, a conversion table exists:

Example

Lets look at an example, 5% Formic Acid and 5% Methanol in an aqueous solution, with the following acute oral toxicity values:
Formic acid: Acute Oral Toxicity, category 3;
Methanol: Acute Oral Toxicity, category 3;
The concentration is given as the weight percentages (5% for each). Lets calculate the right hand side:
= 0.05/100 + 0.05/100 = 0.1/100
And now we can find the ATEmix value, by dividing 100 with this: 100/(0.1/100) = 1000
Looking this value up in the threshold table, we see that this lies in category 4, and hence the mixture will be classified as a category 4 acute oral toxicant.
